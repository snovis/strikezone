What You Need

Cheddar Bob: Bush League is designed to be played anywhere with minimal equipment:

  • Two six-sided dice (2d6) per player, or share one pair
  • Your hands for strategy selection (like rock-paper-scissors)
  • Optional: A scorecard to track the game

Dice Apps Work Great

If you don't have physical dice, any dice-rolling app on your phone works perfectly.

Game Overview

Cheddar Bob: Bush League is a fast-playing baseball simulation for two players. One player is the Pitcher (defense) and the other is the Batter (offense). Players alternate roles after each half-inning.

Objective

Score more runs than your opponent over the course of the game. A standard game is 3 innings, but you can play any number you agree on.

The Core Concept

Every at-bat is a contest between pitcher and batter. Both players simultaneously choose a strategy, then roll dice to determine who wins. The winner rolls again to determine the specific outcome.

Each at-bat follows four steps:

Step 1: COMMIT

Both players secretly choose one of three strategies: Power, Balance, or Finesse. Hold your choice behind your back or under the table.

Step 2: SHOW

On a count of three, both players reveal their choices simultaneously (like rock-paper-scissors). Compare strategies to determine if either player gets a modifier.

Step 3: BATTLE

Both players roll 2d6 and add any modifiers. The player with the higher total wins the battle. Ties are re-rolled.

Step 4: RESULT

The battle winner rolls 2d6 again. Cross-reference their battle tier with their result tier on the appropriate table to determine the outcome.

The Battle Phase

After revealing strategies, both players roll 2d6 and add their modifiers:

  1. Both players roll 2d6
  2. Add +2 if you won the strategy comparison
  3. Compare totals ‚Äî higher wins
  4. If tied, both re-roll (no modifiers on re-roll)

The winner of the battle will roll again in the Result phase. Their battle roll determines which tier they achieved.

The outcome tables are two-dimensional. Your battle tier determines the column, and your result tier determines the row.

Critical Rolls

Rolling the minimum (2) or maximum (12) on 2d6 triggers special outcomes that override normal rules.

During the Battle Phase

Roll Batter Pitcher
Snake Eyes (2) Auto OUT Auto Walk
Boxcars (12) Auto HR Auto DP*

*Double play requires runners on base. If no runners, it's just an out.

During the Result Phase

Critical rolls in the result phase give you the best or worst outcome for your position on the table:

  • Snake Eyes (2): Worst possible outcome for that battle tier
  • Boxcars (12): Best possible outcome for that battle tier

Example

The batter wins a Solid battle (rolled 8). They roll again for the result and get boxcars (12).

Looking at the Batter table, Solid column ‚Äî the best outcome is 2B (from the Strong row). Boxcars gives them a double!

Runner Movement

On Hits

  • Single (1B): R1 ‚Üí 2nd, R2 ‚Üí 3rd, R3 ‚Üí scores
  • Double (2B): R2 & R3 score, R1 ‚Üí 3rd
  • Home Run: All runners score, batter scores

On Walks

Only forced runners advance. A runner is forced if there's a runner (or the batter) taking the base behind them.

Runner Challenge (O-RC)

When the outcome is O-RC, each runner may choose to attempt advancing:

  1. The runner declares they're going
  2. Both players roll 2d6 (no modifiers)
  3. Higher roll wins (ties re-roll)
  4. Runner wins ‚Üí Safe at next base
  5. Defense wins ‚Üí Runner is OUT

Risk vs. Reward

The Runner Challenge is optional. The runner can stay put. But if they try and fail, that's another out!

12

Productive Outs

When the batter wins the battle but the outcome is still an OUT (from the Batter result table), runners advance one base anyway.

The batter fought well and sacrificed themselves to move the runners ‚Äî like a deep fly ball or a productive groundout.

Important

Productive outs only apply to outs from the Batter table, not outs from the Pitcher table. The pitcher's outs are defensive stops, not productive sacrifices.

Game Structure

Innings

A standard game is 3 innings. Each inning has two halves:

  • Top: Away team bats, Home team pitches
  • Bottom: Home team bats, Away team pitches

Outs

Each half-inning continues until the batting team records 3 outs. Then teams switch roles.

Winning

The team with more runs after all innings wins. If the home team is ahead entering the bottom of the last inning, they don't bat ‚Äî they've already won.

Ties

If tied after the final inning, play extra innings until one team leads at the end of a complete inning.
