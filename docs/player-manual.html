Why Cheddar Bob?

One fall, just after the World Series ended, I found myself reflecting on how much I love watching a good Game Seven. There's nothing quite like an entire season coming down to the last inning, of the last game, and the last at-bat.

I had never really seen a baseball game capture the emotional sense of a pitcher-batter matchup. Most games were either way too complex ‚Äî taking hours to play and a PhD in statistics to figure out ‚Äî or they focused too much on batting and pitching separately. You didn't get a sense that you were in charge of playing a game.

So I started working on a fun little pitching simulator. I told my oldest son Ryan about it. Ryan played D1 baseball ‚Äî he was a standout center fielder. I asked him to think about batting and hitting strategy. He was standing in the kitchen when he said, "Well, if you're facing Cheddar Bob..."

I laughed out loud. "Cheddar Bob?"

"Yeah," he responds. "You know, generic fastball guy."

Cheddar Bob was slang for that player who had a big arm in high school, but upon arriving at college learned he was just one of many pitchers who threw hard. I knew I had to call the game Cheddar Bob. That's baseball ‚Äî taking your strengths and humbling them.

Shortly after, my other son Matthew ‚Äî who also played D1 college baseball ‚Äî started brainstorming about the game. He liked the concept. So he played it with a friend from little league. They liked the feel of it, but it took them forty-five minutes to play a single inning. And it was three up, three down. The game was too slow.

Matt wanted something fun, something energizing. He wanted a game he could play with friends in the bullpen, on the bus, or in the locker room. We streamlined the game, and what you hold in your hands is the result. Instead of only generating the tension of the pitcher-batter matchup, we managed to get much more ‚Äî a fast, fun baseball simulation that feels like playing baseball!

Tested & Tuned

Cheddar Bob wasn't just designed ‚Äî it was validated. We simulated tens of thousands of games, tweaking the mechanics until the outcomes matched real baseball. The balance between pitcher and batter? Dead even. The way runs score, the rhythm of innings, the tension of a close game ‚Äî it all checks out against actual MLB statistics.

We're not saying Cheddar Bob is baseball. But when you play it, you'll swear it feels like baseball. And we had so much fun playing it with friends, we wanted to share it with baseball enthusiasts just like you!

What You Need

Cheddar Bob: Bush League is designed to be played anywhere with minimal equipment:

  • Two six-sided dice (2d6) per player, or share one pair
  • Your hands for strategy selection (like rock-paper-scissors)
  • Optional: A scorecard to track the game

Dice Apps Work Great

If you don't have physical dice, any dice-rolling app on your phone works perfectly. Each player can use their own phone.

Game Overview

Cheddar Bob: Bush League is a fast-playing baseball simulation for two players. One player is the Pitcher (defense) and the other is the Batter (offense). Players alternate roles after each half-inning.

Objective

Score more runs than your opponent over the course of the game. A standard game is 3 innings, but you can play any number you agree on.

The Core Concept

Every at-bat is a contest between pitcher and batter. Both players simultaneously choose a strategy, then roll dice to determine who controls the outcome. The winner then rolls again to determine the specific results of the at bat (hit, out, walk, etc.). Cheddar Bob is a game that lets you make baseball decisions, take risks, and try to balance strategy and luck, just like in the "real game."

Key Insight

Unlike traditional baseball simulations, both players are always engaged. Every decision is contested. The pitcher isn't passive ‚Äî they're actively trying to out-think and out-roll the batter on every turn.

The Turn Structure

Each at-bat follows four steps:

Step 1: COMMIT

Both players secretly choose one of three strategies: Power, Balance, or Finesse. Hold your choice behind your back or under the table.

Some players find it easier to think: Power is a rock, paper is balanced, and it takes finesse to run with scissors.

Step 2: SHOW

On a count of three, both players reveal their choices simultaneously (like rock-paper-scissors). Compare strategies to determine if either player gets a modifier.

Step 3: BATTLE

Both players roll 2d6 and add any modifiers they won in the SHOW step. The player with the higher total wins the battle. Ties are re-rolled (with modifiers).

The result of the battle roll also tells the winner what tier they will roll against in the Result phase. A weak win? Use the weak column. A strong win? Your results will come from the strong tier.

Step 4: RESULT

The battle winner rolls 2d6 again (no modifiers ‚Äî straight roll). Cross-reference their battle tier with their result tier on the appropriate table to determine the outcome.

Strategy Triangle

  • Power beats Finesse ‚Äî Raw strength overpowers precision
  • Finesse beats Balance ‚Äî Precision exploits predictability
  • Balance beats Power ‚Äî Adaptability counters aggression

The winner gets +2 to their battle roll. If both choose the same strategy, neither gets a modifier.

Hand Signals

Use these hand signals to reveal your choice:

  • Power (üî®): Closed fist
  • Balance (‚öñÔ∏è): Flat hand, palm down
  • Finesse (üéØ): Pointed finger(s)

The Battle Phase

After revealing strategies, both players roll 2d6 and add their modifiers:

  1. Both players roll 2d6
  2. Add +2 if you won the strategy comparison
  3. Compare totals ‚Äî higher wins
  4. If tied, both re-roll (keep your modifiers)

The winner of the battle will roll again in the Result phase. Their battle roll determines which tier they achieved.

Tier System

Both the battle roll and result roll are converted into tiers using the same thresholds:

Roll Total Tier Meaning
2‚Äì6 Weak Below average performance
7‚Äì9 Solid Average, competent performance
10‚Äì12 Strong Excellent performance

The Magic Number: 7‚Äì9

The most common roll on 2d6 is 7. Rolls of 7, 8, or 9 are "Solid" ‚Äî right in the middle. This is the baseline. Below is Weak, above is Strong.

Critical Rolls

Rolling the minimum (2) or maximum (12) on 2d6 triggers special outcomes that override normal rules.

During the Battle Phase

Roll If Batter Rolls It If Pitcher Rolls It
Snake Eyes (2) Automatic OUT Automatic Walk (BB)
Boxcars (12) Automatic HOME RUN Automatic Double Play*

*Double play requires runners on base. If no runners, it's just an out.

During the Result Phase

Critical rolls in the result phase jump straight to the best or worst outcome on the table:

  • Snake Eyes (2): Worst outcome ‚Äî OUT (batter) or BB (pitcher)
  • Boxcars (12): Best outcome ‚Äî HR (batter) or DP (pitcher)

Outcomes Explained

Hit Outcomes

Outcome What Happens
1B (Single) Batter reaches first base. Runners advance one base.
2B (Double) Batter reaches second base. Runners on 2nd and 3rd score. Runner on 1st goes to third.
HR (Home Run) Batter scores. All runners score.

Walk

Outcome What Happens
BB (Walk) Batter goes to first. Only forced runners advance (runner on 1st goes to 2nd only if batter takes 1st, etc.)

Out Outcomes

Outcome What Happens
O-RA (Runners Advance) Batter is out. All runners advance one base (like a sacrifice fly or groundout).
O-RC (Runner Challenge) Batter is out. Each runner may choose to attempt advancing (see Runner Challenge).
O-RF (Runners Freeze) Batter is out. Runners cannot advance ‚Äî they stay where they are.
DP (Double Play) Two outs recorded. Lead runner is removed from the bases.

Runner Movement

On Hits

  • Single (1B): Runner on 1st ‚Üí 2nd, Runner on 2nd ‚Üí 3rd, Runner on 3rd ‚Üí scores
  • Double (2B): Runners on 2nd and 3rd score, Runner on 1st ‚Üí 3rd
  • Home Run: All runners score, batter scores

On Walks

Only forced runners advance. A runner is forced if there's a runner behind them or the batter taking a base behind them.

Runner Challenge (O-RC)

When the outcome is O-RC, each runner may choose to attempt advancing:

  1. The runner declares they're going
  2. Both players roll 2d6 (no modifiers)
  3. Higher roll wins (ties re-roll)
  4. Runner wins ‚Üí Safe at next base
  5. Defense wins ‚Üí Runner is OUT

Risk vs. Reward

The Runner Challenge is optional. The runner can choose to stay put. But if they try and fail, that's another out!

Double Steals

If there's more than one runner, both can attempt to steal ‚Äî this means two throws and two challenge rolls. It's up to the offensive player whether one runner stays put (like holding a runner on third while the runner on first steals second).

Productive Outs

When the batter wins the battle but the outcome is still an OUT, runners advance one base anyway. The batter fought well and sacrificed themselves to move the runners ‚Äî like a deep fly ball or a productive groundout.

This only applies to outs from the Batter table, not from the Pitcher table.

Game Structure

Innings

A standard game is 3 innings. Each inning has two halves:

  • Top of the inning: Away team bats, Home team pitches
  • Bottom of the inning: Home team bats, Away team pitches

Outs

Each half-inning continues until the batting team records 3 outs. Then teams switch roles.

Winning

The team with more runs after all innings wins. If the home team is ahead going into the bottom of the last inning, they don't need to bat ‚Äî they've already won.

Ties

If the score is tied after the final inning, play extra innings until one team is ahead at the end of a complete inning.

Suggested Game Lengths

How to Keep Score

Keeping score in Cheddar Bob is simple. Just write the outcome code in the batter's box for each at-bat.

Common Notations

Write Meaning Example

2B Double Batter reached second base

R Run scored Circle or underline to mark a run

Tracking Runners

For each at-bat, you can note what happened to runners:

  • Draw a small diamond and fill in bases occupied
  • Or just write the result and update the linescore
  • Circle any notation where a run scored

Some sets include a play mat and tokens to mark runners on base. Players can also use small dice (12mm) to track score ‚Äî when there's no score, the dice stay in the tin. When a team scores, put the die showing their score by their side of the "dugout." (It's super rare to score more than 6 runs in 2‚Äì3 innings of Cheddar Bob.)

The Scrimmage Rule

In more advanced games, players have little trouble tracking scores and outs. But we always encourage the scrimmage rule: both players must agree on the game state. If they don't? RPS or a battle roll to decide!

Scoring Example

Inning 1: Batter 1 walks (BB), Batter 2 singles moving runner to 2nd (1B), Batter 3 hits a home run scoring everyone (HR with circle).

Linescore

At the bottom of each scorecard is a linescore. After each half-inning, write the number of runs scored in that inning. Add them up at the end for the final score.
